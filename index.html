Brand
Guidelines

This brand guidelines page provides an outline of the visual and verbal elements within the Lightmatter Brand.

It is a living, breathing page that helps explain how the various elements within the identity should be used through examples and applications. By following these guidelines, we can ensure all creative work feels cohesive.

Brand guidelines PDF · brand@lightmatter.co · Figma library available

Logo

Lightmatter’s wordmark and monogram closely aligns with the behavior of photonic computing through a typographic interpretation on inference. The aesthetic of the custom type in the letters “L” and “M” resemble a unique yet familiar form when compared to their native letterform. It intrigues our minds to make the connection, straddling both the familiar with the unfamiliar. Further, it helps us recognize how the unknown can be realized.

Logomark

The monogram should be used as the main logo. The 1:2 ratio encourages flexibility across various sizes and areas. It is intended for use on all applications on both print and digital mediums.

Never alter the scale and spacing around or between the monogram, its coloration or form. Do not redraw or alter its original measurements or thickness. Use the white logo on red backgrounds, the red logo on white backgrounds, and the white logo on black backgrounds.

Color

Using color for the identity is not subjective. It’s an important element within the Lightmatter identity. Our colors are a defining aspect of our brand and help us stand out in our field. In addition to the neutral colors, we have a range of distinctive secondary and tertiary colors that add vibrancy and dynamism to support our primary palette. To be effective, one needs to follow the established usages.

Type

Two typefaces carry the brand. Inference Sans is our ownable display face for headlines and big brand moments; Object Sans does everything else: sub-headlines, body copy, captions, and UI.

Download

Primary typeface

Inference Sans

Our ownable, unique typeface: a playful interpretation of inference, replacing letters with distinct forms and glyphs. Use it for headlines, large-scale typography, and the logo. Never set it as body copy, or in sentences longer than 10 words.

Alternates system

Swap letters for their alternate forms to echo how our minds make connections. Keep originals and alternates interchangeable and legible. As a rule, use about one alternate per word, and skip short words where a single swap would dominate. Don’t use the alternate “L”; that curved form is reserved for the logo.

Secondary typeface

Object Sans

Our secondary typeface, with characteristics close to Inference Sans. It carries everything else: sub-headlines, body copy, captions, and UI. Don’t use it for hero or big brand moments.

Icons

In line with our personality, all Lightmatter iconography uses an 8-bit style. Keep details to a minimum. Simplicity makes each icon’s meaning easier to understand. Use icons thoughtfully and intentionally. They are mainly for internal programs and product UI. Use them to support navigation or explain an idea. Do not use them as casual decoration or as a default replacement for other visuals. Do not use them in external marketing unless approved.

Imagery

These four directions are starting points for campaign photography. They are meant to guide image selection, not define an exhaustive or expensive library. Use discretion when choosing imagery. Select high-quality images that feel intentional, ownable, and aligned with the Lightmatter brand. For high-reach campaigns or prominent placements, seek brand approval before use. Each direction should express movement from here to there and the dramatic journey photonics makes possible.

Direction 1

Pivotal Movement

Technology is a pivotal component of human advancement. This direction highlights space through magnificent man-made technology, showcasing our constant progress forward.

Direction 2

Expansive Portal

Images should visualize expansive movement through a portal, bringing us from here to there. This figurative concept conveys wonder, movement, and a physical embodiment of the dramatic journey made possible by photons.

Direction 3

Light Travel / Point A to B

Images should highlight a path from here to there. They show movement through light and make photons feel visible to the naked eye. The goal is a spectacular, lasting impression of technology in the world at large.

Direction 4

A Giant Leap

Images should represent revolutionary moments and figures in history, such as Neil Armstrong, Nikola Tesla, and Claude Shannon. This direction is a literal take on A Giant Leap. It highlights moments in science and technology that changed how we live, think, and understand the world. Close crops should make the viewer feel near the moment. They should build curiosity and excitement, tying back to Pivotal Movement.

Trademarks

Trademark symbols provide legal notice, but they aren’t required on every instance. Mark the first prominent use in each piece, and let the attribution line handle the rest.

The ® symbol identifies a registered trademark. The ™ symbol identifies an unregistered trademark. Used consistently, they help protect Lightmatter’s brands and technologies.

Using the marks

Two rules cover almost everything. Mark the first or most prominent use, skip the symbol after that, and add the attribution line somewhere in the piece.

On first use, pair a trademark with a product or technology name, for example, “Passage® interconnect.” After that, it may be used on its own.

Rule 01

Mark the first instance

Mark the first or most prominent use of a trademark, then drop the symbol on every use after that in the same piece.

Rule 02

Add the attribution line

Include the approved attribution line once per piece, in the footer, closing slide, product docs, or terms of use. It names every Lightmatter trademark in one place.

Logos

Lightmatter logo

  • Keep the LM logo and Lightmatter logo clean everywhere, including the site header, footers, product dashboards, and slide masters.
  • Logos generally do not require a ® or ™ symbol. The attribution line provides trademark notice.
Slides

Presentations

  • Mark each mark’s first use, usually on the title or first content slide. Don’t repeat it after.
  • Mark a standalone slide too, if it might be screenshotted or shared on its own elsewhere.
  • Put the attribution line on a closing legal slide.
Website

Web pages

  • Mark the first use in body copy, then drop the symbol on the rest of the page.
  • Nav, headings, and titles can stay clean, including a hero title like “Passage.”
  • Keep the attribution line in the global footer.
Product

Engravings & packaging

  • The LM logo and product name don’t need a ® or ™ symbol on the engraving. It is ok to leave it off the engravings to keep things clean.
  • The attribution line ships with the product instead, on the packaging, datasheet, or an included card, and provides the notice.

Quick reference

Logos No symbol on logos. The attribution line covers them.
Slides Mark the first or most prominent slide per deck. Attribution line on a closing legal slide.
Website Mark the first use per page. Attribution line in the global footer.
Product Optional on the product. The attribution line on packaging, datasheet, or docs provides the notice.
